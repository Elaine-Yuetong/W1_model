# Free Cash Flow 

## What it is

### 1. Basic Definition

Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures

It answers: *"after running the business and keeping the assets in working order, how much cash is actually left over?"*

### 2. Why This Metric Matters (vs Accounting Profit)

| Concept | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| Net Income / EBITDA | Accounting constructs — include non-cash items, exclude working capital movements, influenced by accounting policy choices |
| Free Cash Flow | Closer to economic reality — reflects what physically moved in and out of the company's bank account from operating and investing activities |

A company can report positive net income while burning cash. A company can report positive EBITDA while generating no free cash flow at all. When the two diverge persistently, it is almost always the cash flow that tells the true story.

### 3. Two Components

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Operating Cash Flow (OCF)** | Cash generated by core business activities — collecting from customers, paying suppliers, paying employees, paying taxes — after adjusting net income for non-cash items (D&A, stock-based compensation) and working capital movements (receivables, inventory, payables). It is the cash equivalent of EBITDA, but more conservative because it includes working capital consumption that EBITDA ignores. |
| **Capital Expenditures (Capex)** | Cash payments made to acquire or maintain physical assets — property, plant, equipment, and in some industries technology infrastructure. These are cash outflows that do not appear on the income statement in the period they are paid (they are capitalized and depreciated over time). This is why EBITDA can look healthy while FCF is negative — a company investing heavily in capex burns cash even if it earns well on paper. |

### 4. Implications for Credit Analysis

| FCF State | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Consistently positive** | Can reduce debt, build liquidity, and survive a downturn by drawing on its own cash generation |
| **Consistently negative** | Must continuously access capital markets — issuing debt or equity — to fund the gap. In a benign environment this is manageable. In a stressed environment, when markets tighten and lenders pull back, a company with multiple quarters of negative FCF has no internal source of funding and no external source willing to step in. This is when negative FCF becomes an existential threat. |

For a credit analyst, FCF answers the most direct question: *"is this business self-funding, or does it need to keep borrowing just to sustain itself?"*

### 5. Trend Signal

A company whose free cash flow is positive but declining — from $500M to $300M to $100M over three consecutive years — is telling you that the business is consuming more and more of its operating cash before anything is available for debt service. Even if it has not yet gone negative, the trajectory is the warning. By the time free cash flow turns negative, the deterioration has typically been visible in the trend for 4–6 quarters.

### 6. FCF Margin

FCF Margin = Free Cash Flow / Revenue

This enables comparison across companies of different sizes and across time as a company grows or shrinks. A company with $200M of FCF on $10B of revenue (2% FCF margin) is in a very different position from a company with $200M of FCF on $1B of revenue (20% FCF margin), even though the absolute cash generation is identical.


## Why it signals stress

Free cash flow signals stress through four mechanisms, each operating on a different timeline and with a different relationship to the other metrics in this spec.

### Mechanism 1 — The self-funding test (immediate)

- A company with negative free cash flow is not self-funding. Every quarter it runs negative FCF, it must find external capital — new debt, new equity, asset sales, or drawing down existing cash reserves — to bridge the gap between what it earns operationally and what it spends.
- This is not automatically a crisis. Companies can run negative FCF for extended periods during investment phases, particularly in capital-intensive industries where large upfront capex precedes years of cash generation.
- The question your system must answer is whether the negative FCF reflects **intentional investment** or **structural deterioration**.

| Type | Accompanying Signs |
|------|---------------------|
| Intentional investment FCF deficits | Rising EBITDA, stable or improving coverage, management guidance on when the investment cycle ends |
| Structural deterioration FCF deficits | Falling EBITDA, declining coverage, no credible path back to positive FCF |

- Your system cannot make this distinction from numbers alone — it requires the LLM layer reading management commentary in the MD&A. But the numbers provide the trigger for that deeper investigation.

**The immediate stress signal:**
- When FCF turns negative after a sustained period of positive generation → flag immediately
- The first negative quarter → may be noise
- Two consecutive negative quarters → a pattern
- Three consecutive negative quarters → severe. The company has been drawing on reserves or borrowing for at least nine months. Whatever external funding has been sustaining it is being depleted.

---

### Mechanism 2 — The debt service reality check (medium term)

- Interest coverage and leverage tell you about the relationship between **earnings** and debt.
- Free cash flow tells you about the relationship between **cash** and debt service.
- These are not the same thing. A company can have comfortable interest coverage (EBITDA of 4x interest expense) but still generate no free cash flow after capex, working capital, and taxes. In that case, while the interest payment is technically covered by earnings, the cash to actually make the payment is coming from somewhere other than operations.

**The divergence between coverage and FCF** is one of the most reliable early warning patterns in credit analysis. When a company shows deteriorating FCF alongside stable or healthy coverage ratios, it typically means one of three things:

1. **Capex is rising faster than earnings** (investment risk)
2. **Working capital is consuming cash** (operational risk)
3. **The quality of EBITDA is declining** (accounting risk — earnings are being recognised but not collected as cash)

All three are stress signals that would be invisible if the analyst looked only at leverage and coverage.

**The medium-term stress signal:**
- A persistent gap between EBITDA and operating cash flow — where EBITDA is stable or growing but OCF is declining — indicates working capital deterioration or earnings quality problems.
- **Flag when OCF/EBITDA conversion ratio falls below 70% for two consecutive quarters.** This means the company is only converting 70 cents of every dollar of EBITDA into actual cash, with the remainder trapped in working capital or lost to non-cash adjustments reversing.

---

### Mechanism 3 — The liquidity runway indicator (forward looking)

- Free cash flow, combined with the current cash balance, tells you **how long a company can sustain its current trajectory** before running out of liquidity.
- This is the most forward-looking signal FCF provides and the one most directly relevant to default prediction.

**Example:**
A company with $500M of cash burning $100M of FCF per quarter has approximately five quarters of runway before cash is exhausted — assuming no debt maturities, no covenant breaches, and no deterioration in the burn rate. In practice, all three of those assumptions tend to fail simultaneously in distressed situations: the burn rate accelerates as the business deteriorates, a debt maturity arrives requiring cash, and the deteriorating coverage triggers a covenant.

The runway calculation gives your system a quantitative estimate of how much time exists before a liquidity crisis, which directly informs how urgently the alert should be escalated.

**The forward-looking stress signal:**
- Compute implied quarters of runway = current cash / absolute value of quarterly FCF burn
- **Flag when runway falls below 6 quarters**
- **Escalate to Stress when runway falls below 3 quarters**

This is the metric most directly predictive of near-term default timing.

---

### Mechanism 4 — The upstream signal (earliest warning)

Of all the metrics in this spec, free cash flow compression is typically the **first** to appear in a deteriorating credit story.

**The sequence in most corporate distress cases follows a consistent pattern:**

| Step | Event | What is visible |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| **1** | Free cash flow begins to compress | Revenue slows, costs rise, or capex increases. Visible in cash flow statement before income statement. |
| **2** | EBITDA begins to decline | Revenue and cost pressures flow through to earnings. Leverage starts to rise. Coverage starts to fall. |
| **3** | Balance sheet deteriorates | Cash burn reduces cash reserves, forces additional borrowing. Leverage reaches stress thresholds. |
| **4** | Triggering event occurs | Debt maturity, covenant breach, ratings downgrade — converts latent stress into acute credit event. |

**Free cash flow compression appears in step one.** Leverage and coverage deterioration appear in step two. Balance sheet stress appears in step three. The credit event is step four.

A system that monitors only leverage and coverage is entering the sequence at step two — already one step behind the earliest available signal.

**The earliest warning signal:**
- **A declining FCF trend across 3–4 consecutive quarters — even if FCF remains positive — is the earliest quantitative stress signal in this system.**
- It should trigger a **Watch alert** before any other metric has moved into a stress band.
- This is the primary reason FCF is included alongside leverage and coverage rather than treated as a derivative metric.

| Source | Content |

| **S&P** | Includes funds from operations (FFO) to debt and free operating cash flow to debt as primary ratios in its financial risk profile assessment. Treats cash-based metrics as complementary to and sometimes more important than earnings-based metrics for assessing true debt serviceability. |

## Formula

> Appendix A

---

**Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures**

This is the universal base definition used across rating agencies, investment banks, and academic literature. It is the simplest and most directly extractable version because both components are explicitly disclosed on the cash flow statement and reliably tagged in XBRL.

However, as with leverage and interest coverage, three formula versions are needed depending on analytical purpose and adjustment philosophy.

---

**Formula 1 — Automated Baseline (XBRL / Deterministic)**

```
Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures

FCF Margin = Free Cash Flow / Revenue

OCF / EBITDA Conversion Ratio = Operating Cash Flow / EBITDA
(EBITDA from Formula 1 of Leverage metric)

Operating Cash Flow = us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivities

Capital Expenditures = us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquirePropertyPlantAndEquipment
```

**XBRL tags:**

| Input | Primary XBRL Tag | Fallback Tag |
|---|---|---|
| Operating Cash Flow | `us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivities` | `us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivitiesContinuingOperations` |
| Capital Expenditures | `us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquirePropertyPlantAndEquipment` | `us-gaap:AcquisitionsNetOfCashAcquiredAndPurchasesOfBusinesses` |
| Revenue (for FCF margin) | `us-gaap:Revenues` | `us-gaap:RevenueFromContractWithCustomerExcludingAssessedTax` |
| EBITDA (for conversion ratio) | Derived — see Leverage Formula 1 | — |

**What is excluded:** Proceeds from asset sales, acquisitions, working capital adjustments beyond what is embedded in OCF, maintenance vs growth capex split, lease payments (post-ASC 842 finance lease principal payments are in financing activities and excluded). Purely what the structured filing reports.

**Three derived outputs from Formula 1:**

```
Output 1 — FCF (absolute):
Free Cash Flow = OCF − Capex
Units: millions USD
Signal: negative = company burning cash after sustaining assets

Output 2 — FCF Margin:
FCF Margin = FCF / Revenue × 100
Units: percentage
Signal: declining margin trend even when absolute FCF positive

Output 3 — OCF/EBITDA Conversion Ratio:
Conversion = OCF / EBITDA
Units: ratio (target ≥ 0.70)
Signal: falling conversion reveals working capital deterioration
or earnings quality problems invisible in EBITDA alone
```

**Error handling:** If OCF is null, FCF = null. If Capex is null but OCF is non-null, compute OCF alone and flag: "capex not found — FCF cannot be computed; OCF reported as partial signal." Never treat missing capex as zero — this would produce a falsely optimistic FCF figure equal to full OCF.

---

**Formula 2 — Moody's-Style (Adjusted, requires LLM footnote extraction)**

Moody's uses Retained Cash Flow (RCF) and Free Cash Flow after dividends as its primary cash flow metrics rather than simple OCF minus capex. The adjustments align cash flow with the same pension and lease adjustments applied to EBITDA and debt in Formula 2 of the Leverage metric.

```
Moody's Adjusted FCF =
    Reported Operating Cash Flow
  + Pension cash contributions added back
    (cash pension contributions are deducted from OCF under GAAP
     but Moody's treats them as financing — adds back to OCF)
  − Maintenance Capex
    (Moody's attempts to separate maintenance capex from growth
     capex where disclosed — only maintenance capex is deducted
     for the baseline FCF; growth capex is treated separately)
  − Dividends paid (common and preferred)
    (Moody's defines FCF after dividends as the residual available
     for debt repayment — dividends are a semi-fixed obligation
     for most investment-grade companies)

Moody's Retained Cash Flow (RCF) =
    Reported Operating Cash Flow
  + Pension cash contribution addback
  − Dividends paid
  (RCF excludes capex — measures cash retained before reinvestment)

RCF / Net Debt = cash generation relative to debt burden
(companion ratio to Debt/EBITDA — used in Moody's rating grids)
```

**What LLM must extract from footnotes:**
- Pension cash contributions for the period (from Pension Footnote — "Employer Contributions" table; same note as Leverage Formula 2)
- Maintenance vs growth capex split, if disclosed (from MD&A capital expenditures discussion or PP&E footnote — many companies disclose this voluntarily; many do not)
- Dividends paid (from cash flow statement financing section — semi-structured; usually tagged but verify)

**Note on maintenance vs growth capex:** This is the single most contested input in FCF analysis. Most companies do not formally disclose the split between maintenance capex (spending required to keep existing assets functioning) and growth capex (spending to expand capacity). Where not disclosed, analysts use industry rules of thumb — typically D&A as a proxy for maintenance capex on the grounds that depreciation approximates economic asset consumption. Your system should: (1) use total capex for Formula 1 (most conservative, most auditable), (2) use D&A as a maintenance capex proxy for Formula 2 where the split is not disclosed, and (3) use company-disclosed split where available via LLM extraction from MD&A.

```
Maintenance Capex Proxy (where not disclosed):
Maintenance Capex ≈ Depreciation & Amortization
(from us-gaap:DepreciationDepletionAndAmortization)
Flag: "maintenance capex proxied by D&A — actual split not
disclosed; growth capex not separately identified"
```

> **Phase 2 note:** This proxy is NOT applied in Phase 2. Phase 2 uses total capex for Formula 1 only. The maintenance vs growth split (including the D&A proxy) is Phase 3 only, when LLM is available to detect whether the company discloses the split.

---

**Formula 3 — S&P-Style (Conservative, requires LLM footnote extraction)**

S&P uses Free Operating Cash Flow (FOCF) and Discretionary Cash Flow (DCF) as its primary cash-based metrics. These are more conservative than Formula 1 because they include additional deductions.

```
S&P Free Operating Cash Flow (FOCF) =
    Funds From Operations (FFO)
  − Changes in Working Capital
  − Capital Expenditures (total, not split)

S&P Funds From Operations (FFO) =
    Net Income
  + D&A
  + Deferred Taxes
  + Other Non-Cash Items
  − Gains on Asset Sales
  [Note: FFO is S&P's version of adjusted OCF — it starts
   from net income rather than operating cash flow, then
   adjusts for non-cash items. It is more conservative than
   OCF because it excludes working capital movements that
   can temporarily inflate OCF]

S&P Discretionary Cash Flow (DCF) =
    FOCF
  − Dividends (common and preferred)
  − Share buybacks (if considered recurring)
  [DCF represents cash truly available for debt repayment
   after all cash obligations including capital returns]

FOCF / Debt = primary S&P cash flow leverage ratio
DCF / Debt = most conservative S&P cash flow metric
```

Source: S&P Corporate Methodology: Ratios and Adjustments (maalot.co.il); S&P defines FFO, FOCF, and DCF as its three primary cash flow metrics and uses FOCF/Debt and DCF/Debt alongside Debt/EBITDA in the financial risk profile assessment.

**What LLM must extract from footnotes:**
- Deferred tax expense / benefit (from income tax footnote — semi-structured, sometimes tagged)
- Gains on asset sales (from income statement and MD&A — same as Leverage Formula 2 non-recurring gains)
- Dividends paid (from financing section of cash flow statement — semi-structured)
- Share buybacks classified as recurring vs opportunistic (from MD&A capital allocation discussion — unstructured; LLM judgment required)

**Key difference from Moody's:** S&P starts from net income (bottom of income statement) and builds up to FFO, then deducts working capital and capex to reach FOCF. Moody's starts from reported OCF (already includes working capital) and makes targeted adjustments. For most companies the two approaches converge to similar numbers, but for companies with large deferred tax movements or significant non-cash items, the starting point matters materially.

---



**Implementation Guidance**

Formula 1 is the automated baseline for Phase 2. Both OCF and capex are among the most consistently tagged items in the cash flow statement — extraction reliability is high. The three derived outputs (absolute FCF, FCF margin, OCF/EBITDA conversion) provide more diagnostic information than a single ratio and should all be computed and stored together.

The OCF/EBITDA conversion ratio deserves particular attention in Phase 2. It requires no additional data beyond what is already extracted for Leverage Formula 1 (EBITDA) and FCF Formula 1 (OCF), and it provides the earliest signal of working capital deterioration or earnings quality problems. A company whose conversion ratio is falling from 0.90 to 0.75 to 0.60 over three quarters is telling you that EBITDA is becoming less and less representative of actual cash generation — which is the earliest warning the structured data can provide before FCF itself turns negative.

Formulas 2 and 3 are reserved for Phase 3. The maintenance vs growth capex split (Formula 2) and the FFO construction (Formula 3) both require LLM footnote extraction and analyst judgment that are not appropriate for the Phase 2 MVP. Once implemented, the divergence between Formula 1 FCF and Formula 3 DCF for the same company is a direct measure of how much of the company's cash generation is being consumed by dividends and capital returns — itself a signal of financial flexibility.

## Where it lives

---

**Formula 1 — Automated Baseline**

All items are structured, on the face of financial statements, available in both 10-K and 10-Q.

| Input | Financial Statement | Exact Line Item | Available In |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operating Cash Flow | Cash Flow Statement — Operating Activities section | "Net cash provided by (used in) operating activities" — the subtotal at the bottom of the operating section | 10-K and 10-Q |
| Capital Expenditures | Cash Flow Statement — Investing Activities section | "Purchases of property, plant and equipment" or "Capital expenditures" or "Additions to property and equipment" | 10-K and 10-Q |
| Revenue (for FCF margin) | Income Statement | "Net revenues" or "Total revenues" or "Net sales" | 10-K and 10-Q |
| EBITDA (for conversion ratio) | Derived — see Leverage Formula 1 | Operating Income + D&A from cash flow statement | 10-K and 10-Q |

**Note on Operating Cash Flow location:** OCF is the subtotal line at the end of the operating activities section of the cash flow statement — not an individual line item within it. It aggregates net income, all non-cash adjustments (D&A, SBC, deferred taxes), and all working capital movements. It is one of the most reliably tagged items in XBRL because it is a required subtotal under US GAAP.

**Note on Capital Expenditures location:** Capex lives in the investing activities section of the cash flow statement, not the operating section. It is presented as a cash outflow (negative number). Companies label it differently — "purchases of PP&E," "capital expenditures," "additions to fixed assets," "investment in property and equipment" — but it always appears in investing activities. Some companies bundle maintenance and growth capex into a single line; others separate them. Your system should take the total unless a split is explicitly disclosed.

**Note on software and intangible capex:** Some companies — particularly technology companies — capitalise internally developed software costs as an intangible asset rather than PP&E. This spending appears in investing activities as "capitalized software development costs" or "purchases of intangible assets" rather than under the PP&E line. For these companies, using only the PP&E capex tag understates total capital investment. Flag when `us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquireIntangibleAssets` is material relative to PP&E capex.

## Extraction Fallback Logic — Free Cash Flow Metric

---

### Design Principles

Same four rules as Interest Coverage apply, plus two additional rules specific to Free Cash Flow:

**Rule 1 — Never substitute zero for a missing input.** Missing capex does not mean zero capex. A company with visible fixed assets on its balance sheet that reports zero capex has an extraction failure, not a genuinely capex-free business model. Mark null and log.

**Rule 2 — Try every fallback before giving up.** Exhaust all tag options before escalating to LLM or declaring failure.

**Rule 3 — Log what you used.** Every computed ratio records which tag path was used for each input.

**Rule 4 — Never divide by zero or negative EBITDA for conversion ratio.** Same rule as Interest Coverage denominator protection — if EBITDA is zero or negative, mark conversion ratio undefined and flag explicitly.

**Rule 5 — Partial computation is permitted for FCF but must be flagged.** Unlike Leverage and Coverage where a missing input nullifies the entire ratio, FCF has a meaningful partial result: if Capex is null but OCF is non-null, OCF alone is reported as a partial stress signal. This is because OCF deterioration is itself a credit signal even without the capex component. The partial result must be clearly labeled and must never be used to trigger alerts as if it were a full FCF computation.

**Rule 6 — Sign convention must be verified before computation.** Capex tags are defined as positive numbers in XBRL despite representing cash outflows. OCF tags are positive when cash is generated and negative when cash is consumed. The correct computation is FCF = OCF − Capex (subtracting a positive capex number from OCF). If the system detects a negative capex tag value, flag as "capex tag returned negative value — possible sign convention error; verify before computing FCF."

---

### Input 1 — Operating Cash Flow

**What we are trying to capture:** The net cash generated or consumed by the company's core operating activities during the period, after all working capital movements and non-cash adjustments.

**The messiness:** OCF is one of the most reliably tagged items in XBRL. The primary failure mode is companies with discontinued operations that report OCF separately for continuing and discontinued segments.

```
Step 1 — Try: us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivities
           This is the required GAAP subtotal — virtually always present.
           Use whenever available.

Step 2 — If Step 1 returns null, try:
           us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivitiesContinuingOperations
           WARNING: excludes discontinued operations.
           Flag: "OCF from continuing operations only —
           discontinued operations excluded; OCF may be
           overstated if discontinued segment was cash-consuming
           or understated if it was cash-generating."

Step 3 — If both steps return null:
           Check whether company filed using a non-standard cash
           flow presentation (e.g. direct method rather than
           indirect method).
           Try: us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivitiesDirectMethod
           Flag: "direct method cash flow statement —
           working capital components not separately disclosed
           in standard format."

Step 4 — If all steps return null → log "OCF: no tag found"
           Set OCF = null → FCF = null
           Set conversion ratio = null
           Escalate to manual review — OCF null is rare and
           may indicate a filing format issue rather than
           genuine absence of data.
```

**Discontinued operations handling:**
```
If both us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivities AND
us-gaap:NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivitiesContinuingOperations
are present:

   Use: NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivities (total)
   Store: NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivitiesContinuingOperations
          as supplementary field
   Flag: "company has discontinued operations —
          total OCF used; continuing operations OCF
          stored separately for trend analysis"

Rationale: total OCF reflects actual cash position;
continuing operations OCF reflects ongoing business quality.
Both are analytically useful — store both, use total for FCF.
```

---

### Input 2 — Capital Expenditures

**What we are trying to capture:** All cash paid to acquire or improve physical and intangible long-lived assets during the period.

**The messiness:** This is the most variable extraction in the FCF metric. Labeling differences across companies are more pronounced for capex than for almost any other line item. Additionally, technology companies frequently have material software capitalisation that must be captured separately.

```
Step 1 — Try: us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquirePropertyPlantAndEquipment
           Primary tag — most commonly used.
           Verify sign: tag should return positive value.
           If negative: flag sign convention error.

Step 2 — If Step 1 returns null, try:
           us-gaap:PaymentsForCapitalImprovements
           Flag: "capital improvements tag used — may exclude
           new asset acquisitions; capex may be understated."

Step 3 — If Step 2 returns null, try:
           us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquireOtherPropertyPlantAndEquipment
           Flag: "other PP&E tag used — verify completeness."

Step 4 — If Steps 1–3 all return null, try summing:
           us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquirePropertyPlantAndEquipment
           + us-gaap:PaymentsForCapitalImprovements
           + us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquireOtherPropertyPlantAndEquipment
           (sum only non-null values)
           Flag: "capex summed from multiple PP&E tags —
           verify no double-counting."
```

**Intangible and software capex check (all levels):**
```
After Steps 1–4, regardless of which level succeeded:

Check for: us-gaap:PaymentsToAcquireIntangibleAssets
           us-gaap:PaymentsToDevelopSoftware
           us-gaap:CapitalizedComputerSoftwareAdditions

If any of these tags are non-null AND their sum exceeds
15% of PP&E capex:
   Flag: "material intangible/software capex identified:
          $X million — total capital investment understated
          in Formula 1 FCF; consider adding for this issuer"
   Store intangible capex separately.
   Do NOT add to Formula 1 capex without explicit instruction —
   this would change the metric definition mid-portfolio.
   Add in Formula 2/3 as an adjustment if analyst confirms.
```

**Level 5 — Last structured resort:**
```
Step 5 — If all PP&E tags return null:
           Try to derive capex from PP&E balance sheet movement:
           Estimated Capex ≈
           PP&E (gross, end of period)
           − PP&E (gross, prior period)
           + Asset disposals (if disclosed)

           XBRL tags:
           us-gaap:PropertyPlantAndEquipmentGross (current period)
           us-gaap:PropertyPlantAndEquipmentGross (prior period)
           us-gaap:ProceedsFromSaleOfPropertyPlantAndEquipment
           (add back disposals to get gross additions)

           Flag: "APPROXIMATION — capex derived from PP&E
           balance movement; actual cash capex may differ due
           to timing, acquisitions, and foreign exchange.
           Do not use for alert triggering without manual review."
```

**Level 6 — Extraction failure:**
```
Step 6 — If all steps including PP&E derivation return null:
           Set Capex = null
           Compute partial FCF signal:
           Partial_FCF_Signal = OCF (if non-null)
           Flag: "capex extraction failed — FCF null;
           OCF reported as partial signal only;
           escalate to manual review"
           Do NOT trigger FCF alerts based on OCF alone.
           Do NOT mark Capex = 0.
```

### Input 3 — Free Cash Flow (Derived)

```
FCF = OCF − Capex

Null propagation rules:
  — If OCF is null → FCF = null; no partial computation
  — If Capex is null but OCF is non-null:
       FCF = null
       Partial_Signal = OCF
       Flag: "FCF null — capex missing; OCF = $X million
              reported as partial signal"
  — If both OCF and Capex are non-null → compute FCF normally

Sign interpretation:
  — FCF > 0: company generating cash after sustaining assets
  — FCF = 0: exactly breakeven (rare; flag for verification)
  — FCF < 0: company burning cash; must fund gap externally
             Do NOT suppress negative FCF — it is the signal
```

### Input 4 — FCF Margin (Derived)

```
FCF Margin = FCF / Revenue × 100

Null propagation rules:
  — If FCF is null → FCF Margin = null
  — If Revenue is null → FCF Margin = null
  — If Revenue = 0 → FCF Margin = undefined
    Flag: "zero revenue — FCF margin undefined;
    verify company has commenced operations"
  — If FCF < 0 → FCF Margin is negative
    Do NOT suppress — negative margin is the signal
    Flag: "negative FCF margin — company burning cash
    relative to revenue base"

Trend alert rule:
  If FCF Margin declines for 3 consecutive quarters,
  regardless of whether it is positive or negative,
  flag: "FCF margin declining trend — [Q-2]: X%,
  [Q-1]: Y%, [Q0]: Z%; upstream stress signal"
```

### Input 5 — OCF / EBITDA Conversion Ratio (Derived)

```
Conversion Ratio = OCF / EBITDA

Null propagation rules:
  — If OCF is null → Conversion = null
  — If EBITDA is null → Conversion = null
  — If EBITDA = 0 → Conversion = undefined
    Flag: "EBITDA zero — conversion ratio undefined"
  — If EBITDA < 0 → Conversion = negative or undefined
    Flag: "EBITDA negative — conversion ratio not meaningful;
    EBITDA stress already captured in Leverage and
    Coverage metrics"
  — If OCF < 0 and EBITDA > 0 → Conversion = negative
    Flag: "negative conversion — company converting positive
    EBITDA into negative operating cash flow; severe
    working capital or earnings quality signal"

Threshold alert rule:
  Conversion < 0.70 for 1 quarter:
    Flag: "low OCF/EBITDA conversion — verify working
    capital movements and earnings quality"
  Conversion < 0.70 for 2 consecutive quarters:
    Watch alert
  Conversion < 0.50 for any quarter:
    Flag alert regardless of trend
  Conversion negative for any quarter:
    Stress alert
```

**Check 1 — Capex vs D&A reasonableness**

Compute: Capex / D&A ratio
If Capex / D&A < 0.50:

## Stress Threshold — Free Cash Flow

---

### Structural Difference From Leverage and Coverage Thresholds

Leverage and Coverage thresholds are sourced directly from S&P's published financial risk profile tables (Tables 4.7, 4.8, 4.9) with specific numeric bands per volatility category. Free Cash Flow thresholds are different in two important ways.

**First difference — FCF has no single universal threshold table.** S&P and Moody's use FCF-based ratios (FOCF/Debt, RCF/Debt, FCF/Debt) as companion metrics to Debt/EBITDA rather than as standalone threshold triggers. The absolute level of FCF in dollars is not directly comparable across companies of different sizes — a $100M FCF deficit means something very different for Apple than for a mid-size retailer. This means FCF thresholds must be expressed in relative terms (as ratios or margins) rather than absolute dollar amounts.

**Second difference — the most important FCF signal is often the trend, not the absolute level.** A company with $50M of positive FCF declining from $500M over six quarters is more stressed than a company with −$20M of FCF that has been stable for four quarters in a planned investment cycle. The threshold framework for FCF must therefore include both absolute level triggers and trend triggers, weighted toward trend.

---

### Four Threshold Dimensions

FCF stress thresholds operate across four dimensions simultaneously. All four must be evaluated together — no single dimension is sufficient alone.

---

**Dimension 1 — Absolute FCF Level**

Source: Market convention and rating agency commentary across cross-sector methodologies. No single published table equivalent to S&P Tables 4.7–4.9.

| FCF Status | Condition | Alert Level |
|---|---|---|
| Strongly positive | FCF > 5% of revenue (FCF margin > 5%) | No alert — healthy |
| Moderately positive | FCF margin 2% – 5% | No alert — monitor trend |
| Weakly positive | FCF margin 0% – 2% | Watch — thin margin; vulnerable to deterioration |
| Breakeven | FCF margin approximately 0% (±0.5%) | Watch — no buffer |
| Mildly negative | FCF margin −1% to 0% | Flag — burning cash; verify investment vs structural |
| Materially negative | FCF margin −3% to −1% | Stress — sustained burn rate |
| Severely negative | FCF margin < −3% | Critical — acute liquidity consumption |

**Formula 1 calibration adjustment:** FCF margin computed from Formula 1 (OCF − total capex) will be lower than S&P FOCF margin (which uses maintenance capex only in some formulations) for companies in heavy investment phases. Apply analyst judgment rather than a fixed offset — unlike leverage where a systematic +0.5x adjustment is appropriate, FCF margin differences between formulas are company-specific and driven by capex intensity.

---

**Dimension 2 — FCF Trend (most important dimension)**

Source: Credit analyst convention; consistent with Moody's guidance that "direction and momentum of cash flow are as important as absolute levels" in cross-sector methodology commentary.

| Trend Condition | Quarters | Alert Level |
|---|---|---|
| FCF margin declining | 2 consecutive quarters, still positive | Watch |
| FCF margin declining | 3 consecutive quarters, still positive | Flag — upstream signal |
| FCF margin declining | 4+ consecutive quarters, still positive | Stress — persistent deterioration |
| FCF positive turning negative | First negative quarter after ≥2 positive | Flag — inflection point |
| FCF negative | 2 consecutive quarters | Stress |
| FCF negative | 3+ consecutive quarters | Critical |
| FCF improving from negative | 2 consecutive improving quarters | Downgrade alert one level — improvement signal |

**The 3-quarter rule:** Three consecutive quarters of declining FCF margin — even from strongly positive levels — is an upstream stress signal that should trigger a Watch alert regardless of the absolute level. This is the earliest quantitative warning the FCF metric produces and typically precedes leverage and coverage deterioration by 2–4 quarters.

---

**Dimension 3 — OCF/EBITDA Conversion Ratio**

Source: Investment banking convention; consistent with S&P's emphasis on "quality of earnings" in its corporate methodology. No single published threshold — the ranges below reflect market practice for investment-grade and high-yield analysis.

| Conversion Ratio | Interpretation | Alert Level |
|---|---|---|
| ≥ 0.90 | High quality — EBITDA converting strongly to cash | No alert |
| 0.75 – 0.90 | Normal range — some working capital consumption | No alert — monitor |
| 0.60 – 0.75 | Below normal — working capital or earnings quality concern | Watch |
| 0.50 – 0.60 | Low conversion — investigate receivables, inventory, payables | Flag |
| < 0.50 | Very low — EBITDA significantly overstating cash generation | Stress |
| Negative | OCF negative while EBITDA positive — severe disconnect | Critical |

**Why this dimension matters independently:** A company can have positive FCF and still trigger a conversion ratio alert if it is consuming working capital to generate that FCF. Stretched payables, uncollected receivables, and inventory build-ups can temporarily inflate OCF in one quarter and then reverse sharply in the next. A falling conversion ratio is the earliest warning of this pattern.

---

**Dimension 4 — Cash Runway**

Source: Derived metric — computed from current cash balance and quarterly FCF burn rate. Used in distressed credit analysis and by rating agencies when assessing near-term liquidity.

```
Cash Runway (quarters) =
Current Cash & Equivalents /
Absolute Value of Quarterly FCF Burn

(Only computed when FCF is negative)
(Current Cash from Leverage Formula 1 cash extraction)
(Quarterly FCF Burn = most recent quarter's negative FCF)
```

| Cash Runway | Alert Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| > 8 quarters | Watch — burning cash but runway is adequate | Log; monitor trend |
| 5 – 8 quarters | Flag — runway shortening; refinancing or turnaround needed | Alert; daily monitoring |
| 3 – 5 quarters | Stress — less than 1 year of runway at current burn rate | Immediate alert; escalate |
| < 3 quarters | Critical — acute near-term liquidity risk | Immediate escalation; cross-reference Liquidity and Debt Maturity Wall metrics |
| < 1 quarter | Emergency — potential default within months | Highest priority alert |

**Caveat on cash runway calculation:** This metric assumes the burn rate is constant, which is almost never true. A deteriorating business typically accelerates its burn rate as conditions worsen. The runway estimate is therefore an upper bound — actual time to liquidity exhaustion is likely shorter. Flag all runway calculations with: "runway estimate assumes constant burn rate at most recent quarter — actual runway may be shorter if deterioration continues."

**Additional caveat:** Companies with revolving credit facilities, undrawn committed facilities, or asset sale programmes have additional liquidity beyond cash on hand. Cash runway from cash alone understates total liquidity. Cross-reference with the Liquidity metric for a more complete picture. Flag: "cash runway computed from cash balance only — committed undrawn facilities and other liquidity sources not included; see Liquidity metric."

---

### Combined Alert Trigger Rules

All four dimensions feed into a single alert output per issuer per period. The most severe alert across all four dimensions governs the overall FCF alert level.

| Alert Level | Any of These Conditions | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Watch** | FCF margin 0%–2%; OR FCF margin declining 2 consecutive quarters; OR conversion ratio 0.60–0.75; OR cash runway 5–8 quarters | Log; weekly review |
| **Flag** | FCF margin −1% to 0%; OR FCF positive turning negative (first negative quarter); OR FCF margin declining 3 consecutive quarters; OR conversion ratio 0.50–0.60; OR cash runway 3–5 quarters | Generate alert; daily monitoring |
| **Stress** | FCF margin −1% to −3%; OR FCF negative 2 consecutive quarters; OR FCF margin declining 4+ consecutive quarters; OR conversion ratio < 0.50; OR cash runway < 3 quarters | Immediate alert; escalate to analyst |
| **Critical** | FCF margin < −3%; OR FCF negative 3+ consecutive quarters; OR conversion ratio negative; OR cash runway < 1 quarter | Highest priority alert; cross-reference all other metrics |
| **Trend Reversal (positive)** | FCF improving for 2+ consecutive quarters from negative | Downgrade alert one level; flag improvement in audit log |
| **Extraction Failure** | OCF null after all fallbacks | Do not compute; escalate to manual review |

---

### Sector Exceptions and Adjustments

**Capital-intensive industries in investment phase (utilities, infrastructure, semiconductors, oil & gas during construction):**
Companies deliberately running negative FCF during a capital investment cycle should not be automatically flagged at Stress or Critical without context. The distinguishing feature is whether EBITDA and coverage are stable or improving while FCF is negative — this pattern suggests investment-driven FCF suppression rather than structural deterioration. Apply the following adjustment:

> If FCF is negative AND EBITDA coverage is ≥ 3.0x AND leverage is stable or declining → downgrade FCF alert one level AND flag: "FCF negative in apparent investment cycle — EBITDA and coverage stable; monitor for investment cycle completion."

**Startups and early-stage companies:** These companies structurally burn FCF during growth phases. Your portfolio of corporate bond issuers is unlikely to include genuinely early-stage companies — most bond issuers are established businesses with operating histories. If encountered, flag for manual review rather than applying standard thresholds.

**Financial institutions:** Same sector exception as Leverage and Coverage — FCF as defined here is not applicable to banks and insurance companies. Flag for manual review.

**Seasonal businesses (retail, agriculture, hospitality):** Quarterly FCF can swing dramatically due to seasonality — a retailer will show strongly negative FCF in Q2 and Q3 (inventory build) and strongly positive in Q4 (holiday sales collection). Always compare to the same quarter of the prior year rather than the sequential prior quarter for seasonal issuers. Flag when year-over-year same-quarter FCF deteriorates by more than 20%.

## Signal Timing — Free Cash Flow

---

### Classification

**Earliest of all primary metrics — leading indicator with a structural advantage over leverage and coverage.**

Free cash flow is the only primary metric in this spec that can signal stress before it appears in either the income statement or the balance sheet. This is not a claim about data availability timing — all three metrics are reported on the same filing schedule. It is a claim about economic timing: FCF deterioration typically precedes EBITDA deterioration, which precedes leverage deterioration, in the sequence of corporate distress.

The reason is structural. The cash flow statement captures working capital movements and capital spending that the income statement does not. A company can report stable EBITDA while its receivables are growing uncollected, its inventory is building unsold, and its suppliers are being paid faster. All three of these consume cash without touching the income statement. The OCF/EBITDA conversion ratio detects this pattern. By the time the working capital problem is large enough to affect EBITDA — through bad debt write-offs, inventory write-downs, or supplier payment pressure — the cash flow statement will have been signalling it for 2–4 quarters.

This leading advantage is the primary analytical justification for including FCF as a standalone metric rather than treating it as a derivative of leverage and coverage.

---

### The Distress Sequence and FCF's Position Within It

The typical distress sequence for a corporate issuer — validated across multiple default studies including Altman (1968), Ohlson (1980), and Moody's empirical default research — follows this pattern:

```
Quarter −6 to −4 (earliest signal):
FCF compression begins.
Revenue slows or costs rise.
Working capital begins consuming cash.
OCF/EBITDA conversion ratio starts falling.
FCF margin declines but remains positive.
→ Your system flags: Watch (Dimension 2 — declining trend)

Quarter −4 to −2 (secondary signal):
EBITDA begins declining.
Leverage starts rising.
Coverage starts falling.
FCF turns negative or approaches zero.
→ Your system flags: Flag/Stress on FCF
→ Your system flags: Watch/Flag on Leverage and Coverage

Quarter −2 to 0 (confirming signals):
Balance sheet deteriorates.
Cash reserves deplete.
Debt maturities approach.
Leverage in Aggressive/Highly Leveraged band.
Coverage below 2.0x.
Cash runway < 4 quarters.
→ Your system flags: Stress/Critical across multiple metrics
→ Dual and triple metric alerts trigger

Quarter 0 (credit event):
Covenant breach, rating downgrade, missed payment,
bankruptcy filing, or distressed exchange.
```

FCF enters this sequence at Quarter −6 to −4. Leverage and Coverage typically enter at Quarter −4 to −2. This gives FCF a **2–4 quarter lead advantage** over the other primary metrics in typical distress scenarios.

This lead advantage is why the Phase 3 backtest targets a median lead time of ≥ 3 quarters for FCF compared to ≥ 2 quarters for leverage and coverage.

---

### Three-Tier Timing Structure

| Tier | Source | Data Quality | Lag After Quarter-End | Lead Before 10-Q | Use in System |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Full recompute** | 10-Q / 10-K | Reviewed (10-Q) or audited (10-K); structured XBRL; Formula 1 computable | **+40 days** (large accelerated filer) | n/a | Primary FCF ratio; triggers all alerts |
| **Tier 2 — Early directional signal** | 8-K Item 2.02 earnings press release | Preliminary; unaudited; company-defined; unstructured text | **+14 to +25 days** | **−15 to −25 days before 10-Q** | Directional signal only; Phase 3 only |
| **Tier 3 — Real-time event trigger** | 8-K Items 2.03 / 2.04 | Event disclosure only; no cash flow data | **Within 4 business days** | Any time intra-quarter | 2.04: automatic Stress alert; 2.03: note debt event but no FCF update possible |

**FCF-specific note on Tier 2:** The earnings press release (Item 2.02) is less useful for FCF than for coverage. Press releases typically disclose EBITDA and net income prominently but rarely disclose operating cash flow or capex — these are considered full financial statement disclosures and companies generally do not include them in press releases. The directional FCF signal from Tier 2 is therefore indirect: falling EBITDA in the press release implies likely FCF compression, but the actual OCF and capex figures will not be visible until the 10-Q arrives.

**Exception:** Some capital-intensive companies (energy, utilities, mining) and some high-yield issuers voluntarily disclose FCF in their earnings press releases because it is a key metric for their investor base. For these issuers, Tier 2 provides a genuine FCF directional signal. Your system should check for the keywords "free cash flow," "operating cash flow," and "capital expenditures" in the Item 2.02 text when Phase 3 LLM processing is active.

**FCF-specific note on Tier 3:** Unlike coverage, where a new debt event (Item 2.03) allows a projected interest expense impact to be estimated, a debt event has no direct mechanical impact on FCF that can be computed intra-quarter. The new debt increases cash (temporarily improving the runway calculation) but the interest payments will only appear in OCF in subsequent quarters. Flag the debt event in the context of the FCF runway calculation: updated cash balance from the debt event should be noted as increasing runway, but with the caveat that future interest payments will consume OCF.

### Comparison: FCF vs Leverage vs Coverage Signal Speed

| Scenario | FCF Signal | Coverage Signal | Leverage Signal | Earliest Signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Working capital deterioration | **Fast — 1–2 quarters** | None until EBITDA falls | None until EBITDA falls | **FCF leads by 2–4 quarters** |
| Revenue shock (sudden) | Fast — same quarter | Fast — same quarter | Slow — debt unchanged | FCF and Coverage simultaneously |
| Capex surge (investment) | **Immediate — same quarter** | None | None | **FCF leads** — but may be intentional investment |
| Interest rate rise on refinancing | None directly | Medium — 2–4 quarters | None directly | Coverage leads |
| New debt issuance | Temporary cash increase (positive) | None immediately | **Immediate** | Leverage leads |
| Margin erosion (gradual) | **Medium — 2–3 quarters** | Medium — 2–3 quarters | Medium — 2–3 quarters | FCF slight lead via conversion ratio |
| Simultaneous EBITDA fall + new debt | Fast on conversion ratio | Fast on coverage | Fast on leverage | All three simultaneously — triple alert |

**Key insight from this table:** FCF leads in working capital, capex, and gradual margin deterioration scenarios — which collectively represent the majority of corporate distress cases. Coverage leads in interest rate scenarios. Leverage leads in sudden debt accumulation scenarios. A system monitoring all three covers the full space of stress initiation pathways.

### The Q4 Dark Window — FCF Is Worst Affected

As established in the Leverage and Coverage frequency sections, the Q4 dark window (Q3 10-Q filed ~November 9, 10-K filed ~March 31, gap ~142 days) represents the longest period without structured financial data.

FCF is more severely affected by the Q4 dark window than either leverage or coverage for two reasons:

**Reason 1 — No intra-quarter FCF proxy exists.** For leverage, debt event filings (Item 2.03, 424B) partially illuminate Q4 debt movements. For coverage, the earnings press release (Item 2.02, typically filed late January or early February) discloses EBITDA which is the primary coverage driver. For FCF, neither OCF nor capex is disclosed outside of the formal quarterly filing. The only FCF-relevant Q4 signal is the earnings press release for companies that voluntarily disclose FCF — which is a minority of issuers.

**Reason 2 — Q4 is seasonally the highest FCF quarter for many industries.** Retailers collect holiday season receivables, consumer companies generate peak revenue, and many industrial companies complete annual capex programmes. A Q4 FCF collapse — which would be the most alarming possible signal — is therefore invisible to your system for up to 60 days after fiscal year-end, and not confirmed in structured XBRL until the 10-K is filed.

**System response to Q4 dark window for FCF:**
```
For any issuer flagged at Watch or above on FCF at Q3:
   Activate heightened monitoring during November – March window.
   Monitor for:
     1. 8-K Item 2.04 (debt acceleration) — automatic escalation
     2. 8-K Item 2.02 (earnings press release, ~late January)
        — check for FCF disclosure or EBITDA deterioration
     3. NT 10-K filing (extension request) — itself a mild
        stress signal; flag: "10-K extension requested —
        heightened monitoring; verify no Q4 deterioration"
   Flag in weekly review: "Q4 dark window active for [issuer] —
   last FCF data from [Q3 date]; next structured update
   expected [estimated 10-K date]"
```

---

### Staleness Analysis

Identical staleness structure to Leverage and Coverage — Tier 1 data is between 40 and 182 days old depending on filing timing. Refer to the Leverage Signal Timing section for the full staleness table.

One FCF-specific addition: OCF and capex are cumulative year-to-date figures in 10-Q filings, not quarter-only figures for most companies. This means the Q3 10-Q discloses nine-month cumulative OCF and capex, not just Q3 standalone figures. To compute quarterly FCF, the system must subtract the prior period's cumulative figure:

```
Quarterly FCF = Current period cumulative FCF
              − Prior period cumulative FCF

Example for Q3:
Q3 standalone FCF =
  (9-month OCF − 9-month Capex)
  − (6-month OCF − 6-month Capex)

XBRL period contexts must be checked to verify
whether tags represent quarterly or YTD figures.
Misidentifying YTD as quarterly overstates Q3 FCF
by approximately 3×.
Flag: "quarterly FCF derived by subtracting
prior period cumulative from current period
cumulative — verify period contexts match."
```

### Updated Signal Timing Summary

> **Signal Timing — Earliest of the primary metrics; leading indicator by 2–4 quarters in most distress scenarios.**
>
> Free cash flow is a flow measure that reflects cash movements the income statement does not capture — working capital consumption, capex intensity, and earnings quality. Tier 1 structured data arrives 40 days after quarter-end, on the same schedule as leverage and coverage. The economic lead advantage is not about filing timing — it is about the fact that cash deterioration precedes earnings deterioration in most stress scenarios.
>
> The OCF/EBITDA conversion ratio is the most sensitive early warning signal within the FCF metric — it detects working capital and earnings quality problems 2–4 quarters before they flow through to EBITDA, leverage, and coverage. A declining conversion ratio should trigger a Watch alert even when absolute FCF remains positive.
>
> The Q4 dark window is structurally worse for FCF than for any other metric — no intra-quarter FCF proxy exists, and Q4 is the peak FCF quarter for many industries. Issuers flagged at Watch or above on FCF at Q3 should be placed on heightened monitoring through the November–March window.
>
> FCF, leverage, and coverage are complementary leading and lagging indicators that together cover the full space of stress initiation pathways. FCF leads in working capital and capex scenarios. Coverage leads in interest rate scenarios. Leverage leads in debt accumulation scenarios. All three should always be evaluated together — triple metric deterioration is the strongest quantitative stress signal the structured data can produce.

### FCF-Specific Differences

**Difference 1 — Item 2.02 provides weaker FCF signal than for Coverage**

For coverage, the earnings press release discloses EBITDA — the primary coverage driver — giving a genuine directional signal 14–25 days before the 10-Q. For FCF, press releases rarely disclose OCF or capex. The FCF signal from Item 2.02 is therefore indirect and weaker.

**System behavior in Phase 3 when Item 2.02 is detected:**

```
Step 1 — LLM reads Item 2.02 text
Step 2 — Check for explicit FCF disclosure:
         Keywords: "free cash flow", "operating cash flow",
         "capital expenditures", "cash generation"
         If found: extract figure and store as Tier 2
         preliminary FCF estimate
         Flag: "FCF disclosed in earnings press release —
         preliminary, unaudited, company-defined;
         treat as directional signal only"
Step 3 — If no explicit FCF disclosure:
         Extract EBITDA from press release (if disclosed)
         Store as indirect FCF signal:
         Flag: "no FCF disclosed in press release —
         EBITDA directional signal only; OCF and capex
         not available until 10-Q"
Step 4 — Do NOT attempt to compute FCF from
         press release EBITDA alone — OCF and capex
         are independent of EBITDA and cannot be
         reliably estimated without the cash flow statement
```

**Industries where Item 2.02 FCF disclosure is common:**
Energy (oil & gas, midstream), mining, utilities, and high-yield issuers with investor-relations FCF guidance. For these sectors, activate full Tier 2 FCF processing in Phase 3. For all other sectors, treat Item 2.02 as an EBITDA directional signal only.

---

**Difference 2 — Item 2.03 and 424B have indirect and delayed FCF impact**

For coverage, a new debt event (Item 2.03 or 424B) allows a projected interest expense impact to be computed mechanically from the coupon rate. For FCF, the impact is indirect and delayed:

```
Immediate impact (same quarter):
   Cash balance increases by debt proceeds
   → Improves cash runway calculation
   → Update: Cash Runway = (Prior Cash + New Debt Proceeds)
              / Absolute quarterly FCF burn
   Flag: "cash runway updated for new debt proceeds from
   [8-K / 424B date]; does not reflect future interest
   payments on new debt"

Delayed impact (next quarter and beyond):
   Interest payments on new debt reduce OCF
   → FCF will be lower in subsequent quarters
   → Estimate: Annual interest impact =
     New Debt Amount × Coupon Rate
     Quarterly OCF reduction ≈ Annual interest / 4
   Flag: "forward FCF will be reduced by estimated
   $X million per quarter from new debt interest;
   full impact visible in next 10-Q"

System action:
   Store both the immediate cash runway update and
   the forward FCF impact estimate separately.
   Do NOT adjust the current period FCF figure.
   Do NOT trigger FCF alerts based on forward estimates alone.
   Use forward estimate as context for analyst review only.
```

---

**Difference 3 — Cumulative vs quarterly figures require period subtraction**

As noted in Signal Timing, OCF and capex in 10-Q filings are typically year-to-date cumulative figures, not quarter-only. This creates a frequency-specific extraction requirement that does not apply to leverage or coverage — both of which use balance sheet items (instant) or income statement items that are consistently reported as period-specific figures.

```
Period handling rule for FCF extraction:

For 10-K filings:
   OCF and Capex are full-year figures.
   Use directly — no subtraction needed.
   Period = fiscal year end date.

For Q1 10-Q:
   OCF and Capex are Q1 standalone (3-month YTD).
   Use directly — first quarter has no prior period
   to subtract.
   Period = March 31 (or Q1 fiscal end date).

For Q2 10-Q:
   OCF and Capex may be 6-month YTD.
   Quarterly FCF = (6M OCF − 6M Capex)
                 − (3M OCF − 3M Capex)
   where 3M figures come from Q1 10-Q stored values.
   Period = June 30 (or Q2 fiscal end date).

For Q3 10-Q:
   OCF and Capex may be 9-month YTD.
   Quarterly FCF = (9M OCF − 9M Capex)
                 − (6M OCF − 6M Capex)
   where 6M figures come from Q2 10-Q stored values.
   Period = September 30 (or Q3 fiscal end date).

Verification step:
   Check XBRL period context tags to confirm
   whether values are YTD or quarter-only.
   Some companies tag both — use quarter-only
   if available to avoid subtraction dependency.
   Flag: "quarterly FCF derived by period subtraction
   — verify XBRL period contexts confirmed YTD basis"

Storage requirement:
   System must store cumulative OCF and Capex
   per period to enable Q2 and Q3 subtraction.
   If Q1 filing data is missing from storage
   when Q2 arrives: mark Q2 quarterly FCF as null
   Flag: "prior period cumulative not available —
   quarterly FCF cannot be derived; YTD FCF stored
   as substitute"
   Store YTD FCF as fallback even when quarterly
   FCF cannot be computed.
```

---

**Difference 4 — Annual FCF run-rate calculation**

For trend analysis and cash runway computation, the system needs an annualised FCF figure regardless of which quarter is being processed. This is straightforward for annual 10-K data but requires annualisation for quarterly data.

```
Annual FCF Run-Rate:

From 10-K: use full-year FCF directly
From Q1 10-Q: Annual run-rate = Q1 FCF × 4
              Flag: "annualised from single quarter —
              high variance; seasonal distortion possible"
From Q2 10-Q: Annual run-rate = H1 FCF × 2
              Flag: "annualised from two quarters —
              moderate variance"
From Q3 10-Q: Annual run-rate = (9M FCF / 3) × 4
              Flag: "annualised from three quarters —
              most reliable quarterly estimate;
              Q4 not yet captured"

Preferred approach for trend analysis:
   Use trailing twelve months (TTM) FCF where possible:
   TTM FCF = Full prior year FCF
           + YTD current year FCF
           − Same YTD prior year FCF
   (requires storing prior year quarterly data)
   Flag: "TTM FCF computed from [prior year 10-K]
   and [current year YTD 10-Q]"
```
